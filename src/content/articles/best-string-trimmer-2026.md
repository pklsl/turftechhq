A string trimmer — or "weed eater," as most people call it — might be the hardest-working tool in your lawn care arsenal. It does the detail work that makes the difference between a lawn that looks mowed and one that looks groomed: tight edges along fences, clean borders around beds, trimming under obstacles the mower can't reach.

But with dozens of options across gas, battery, and electric power sources, choosing the right trimmer isn't simple. Power levels vary wildly. Weight matters more than most people realize. And the line feed system — how the trimmer advances fresh cutting line — determines whether a session is pleasant or maddening.

This guide breaks down the best string trimmers across all categories for 2026, with honest pros and cons for each.

## Gas vs. Battery vs. Corded Electric: Which Is Right for You?

Before getting into specific models, the power source decision is the most important choice you'll make.

### Gas String Trimmers

**Gas trimmers** run on a 2-cycle (or occasionally 4-cycle) engine fueled by a gas-oil mix. They offer unlimited run time, maximum power, and professional-grade performance.

**Pros:**
- Most powerful option — can handle heavy, overgrown brush
- No battery management or charging required
- Unlimited runtime for large properties
- Better performance in demanding professional applications

**Cons:**
- Heavier and louder than battery models
- 2-cycle engines require fuel mixing and more maintenance
- More difficult to start (especially in cold weather)
- Emit exhaust fumes
- More vibration than electric alternatives

**Best for:** Large properties (half acre+), heavy-duty trimming applications, professional use.

### Battery String Trimmers

**Battery (cordless) trimmers** have become the dominant choice for residential users in 2026. Modern 40V–82V battery platforms deliver performance that rivals gas in most residential applications, with the added benefits of quiet operation, zero emissions, and easy startup.

**Pros:**
- Near-gas power on premium 56V–82V platforms
- Quiet operation — no noise complaints from neighbors
- No fuel mixing, no emissions
- Instant start — press a button and go
- Less vibration for better operator comfort

**Cons:**
- Limited runtime (typically 30–60 minutes per charge)
- Battery adds weight, especially in backpack or belt-pack configurations
- Battery and charger can add significant cost if not already in an ecosystem

**Best for:** Most residential lawns under half an acre; homeowners who value convenience and low maintenance.

### Corded Electric String Trimmers

**Corded electric trimmers** offer consistent power without battery concerns, at typically the lowest price point.

**Pros:**
- Consistent, unlimited power as long as it's plugged in
- Lightest weight among powered trimmers
- Lowest purchase price
- No battery degradation over time

**Cons:**
- Limited by cord length (typically 50–100 feet)
- Cord management is inconvenient and potentially dangerous
- Not practical for large or complex lawns

**Best for:** Small, simple properties close to power outlets; budget-conscious buyers.

## String Trimmer Buying Guide

### Line Feed System: Bump-Feed vs. Auto-Feed vs. Fixed Line

**Bump-feed:** Tap the head on the ground to advance fresh line. Reliable and widely used, but can inadvertently advance line on uneven ground.

**Auto-feed:** Electronically or mechanically senses when line is short and advances automatically. More convenient, occasionally over-feeds.

**Fixed-line (star-wheel):** Line segments clipped into the head. No automatic advancement — you replace segments when they break. Very common on commercial straight-shaft models. Most reliable and handles heavy growth best.

### Shaft Type: Straight vs. Curved

**Straight shaft:** Longer reach, better for trimming under obstacles, preferred by professionals. Required for most heavy-duty applications and commercial work.

**Curved shaft:** More natural ergonomic position for most users, typically lighter. Fine for standard residential trimming.

### Cutting Swath

A wider cutting swath means fewer passes — but wider heads create more stress on the motor and line. For most residential lawns, **13–17 inches** is the ideal range.
